Context

Sony's retail training problem

Sony needed to modernise how they train retail staff and channel partners across Europe. Product information was being distributed slowly, inconsistently, and at enormous cost — delivered through disparate local materials that couldn't keep pace with Sony's release schedule.

The brief from the client was direct: "We need to get our product information out to our staff and audience as quickly as possible." That meant a centralised platform — one that worked across 15+ countries, supported 11+ languages, and could scale without the overhead of regional production teams rebuilding content from scratch every time.

I was the UX/UI Designer on the project at Let's Create — responsible for the end-to-end design of the platform. That meant the learner-facing course experience, the content authoring tools, the mobile companion app, and the analytics dashboard. No other designer on the project.

The problem

Cost, speed, and consistency — all broken at once

"We need to get our product information out to our staff and audience alike as quickly as possible."

Sony stakeholder, project kick-off

Sony's existing approach had four compounding problems:

  • Content had long lead times — regional teams rebuilt materials locally, introducing delays and version drift
  • No central source of truth — retailers in different countries were working from different versions of the same information
  • No engagement data — Sony couldn't tell whether staff had completed training or retained anything from it
  • Localisation was manual and expensive — translation wasn't built into the workflow, it was bolted on afterwards

Any solution had to solve all four simultaneously. Fixing just one would have moved the bottleneck, not removed it.

What I designed

Four components, one failure point each

The platform wasn't a single product — it was four interconnected surfaces. I designed each one, and each one addressed a different breakdown in Sony's existing workflow.

Design process

Research first, then build

I ran stakeholder interviews with Sony's product and marketing teams at the start of the engagement to understand the full scope of the distribution problem. That research directly shaped the architecture decision — build a centralised authoring tool, not a shared file system. One content source, pushed everywhere.

I then reviewed Sony's existing training materials with users to understand what wasn't working from the learner's side: content was too long, structure was unclear, and staff had no sense of progress or where they were in a course. Those three problems became the core design constraints for the learning experience.

Platform design

Built for scale, designed for clarity

The design had to work across 15+ markets without regional customisation of core UI — every interaction pattern had to be immediately understandable to a retail staff member regardless of language or prior platform experience.

The content authoring environment: Sony's central team uses this to create courses once, configure translations, and publish to all 15+ markets — no regional rebuild required.

Key design decisions

Designing for non-technical users at scale

The platform's primary users were retail staff — people who sell Sony products, not product managers or developers. The design had to be intuitive enough to require no training to use the training platform itself.

Three design principles drove every decision:

  • Clarity over comprehensiveness. Sony's product catalogue is vast. The temptation was to put everything in. We prioritised what retail staff actually needed on the shop floor — key specs, selling points, and course completion, not the full technical datasheet.
  • Progress visibility. Staff needed to know exactly where they were in their training journey. Persistent progress indicators, clear module structures, and completion states were non-negotiable from the first round of user feedback.
  • Localisation as a feature, not a fix. The content architecture was designed around the assumption that all content would exist in multiple languages. Translation wasn't an afterthought — it was built into the authoring workflow from day one.

Three months of post-launch iteration, driven by real engagement data, refined the experience in ways upfront research couldn't predict. The analytics dashboard wasn't just a deliverable — it was what made the product improvable.

Scope and scale

11 languages, 15 countries, one design system

One of the more technically demanding design challenges was ensuring the UI held up across all supported languages — including languages with significantly longer word lengths than English. Every component was stress-tested against the most verbose language variant to prevent layout breakage.

SCORM 1.2 and SCORM 2004 compatibility was a hard requirement for enterprise compliance tracking. The course UI was designed to work within SCORM's constraints without compromising the learning experience — keeping interactions accessible and navigation predictable even inside the SCORM wrapper.

What I took away

Scale changes the design problem

Working on a multinational platform taught me that design decisions you'd make for a single-market product often don't survive contact with 15 markets. The version that ships globally is rarely the most locally-polished — it's the most structurally resilient.

The most important thing I learned: the analytics dashboard wasn't just a reporting tool. It was what made the platform improvable. Without usage data, post-launch iteration is guesswork. Building measurement in from the start — and designing an interface that made that data readable to non-technical stakeholders — was as important as anything in the learner-facing UI.
